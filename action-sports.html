Action Sports - Hawke's Bay

Action Sports

What is Action Sports?

Action Sports is a list of assorted activities which usually include a higher degree of risk and/or technical skill. This could be anything from Mountain Biking, to Snowboarding, to Rock Climbing, although this website focuses primarily on Action Sports which can be enjoyed more casually and in an urban environment and that have a more popular audience, particularly in Hawkes Bay.

These sports are Skateboarding, Scootering, BMX, Roller-Skating, Aggressive Skating, Team-Based (Roller Derby and Inline Hockey) and Niche sports (Downhill and Artistic).


What are Styles?

Styles are the “flavour”, so to speak, of the action sport. They differ in the environment which it is ridden in, the tricks that are predominantly used, and even the cultures that follow them. These styles, which were created during the development of skateboarding, transcend all Action Sports. Although each sport brings their own unique styles, the main styles shared across all sports are:


  • Street

Street style was created in the decline of mainstream interest in skateboarding in the late 80’s and early-to-mid 90’s. Because of this decline, less skateparks were being built and maintenance of this parks slowed down, so skaters took to the streets. With an abundance of features in public areas, skaters adapted to incorporate these features into their sport. Main features of this style include rails (or handrails), stair sets, benches, banks, and almost any other feature found in public areas. This change in environment also spurred a change in skateboarding manoeuvres, to better incorporate these new obstacles. In street, tricks such as flips and grinds were greatly expanded, as the new obstacles allowed for more focused and technical manoeuvres.

  • Vert

Vert, which is short for “vertical”, was hugely popular in the 70’s and 80’s, as a child of surfer styles of skating from the 60’s, who skated drained swimming pools and drainage pipes. Those obstacles directly influenced the style without almost any design changes, primarily focused on bowls (pools) and half-pipes (drainage pipes). Due to the nature of the obstacles, this style involves the rider moving fast up an incline (instead of on flat ground) and being in the air (the term used is “airtime”). The main manoeuvres developed around this were grabs (grabbing the board with your hand in the air), rotations (spinning of the body and/or board) and plants (using your hands or feet hold yourself at the top of a quarter-pipe or bowl with a combination of grabs and/or rotations).

  • Park

Typically found in skateparks, hence the name “Park”, these environments involve designs of Street and Vert features to create unique structures for the rider. Focusing less on single obstacles or tricks, park demands more flow (combination of speed and direction) and adaptation as no two skateparks are the same. This style can also be referred to as tranny (short for “transitional”).

Skateboarding

Short History: The true origin of skateboarding isn’t held by any single person, but rather a community. Surfers in 1950’s California used skateboards (which then were homemade, made up of wooden planks with clay roller skates wheels attached) to simulate their surfing in the streets when they were unable to surf.

From that point onwards, skateboarding slowly went through developments throughout the decade to become what it is today. To summarise, the biggest changes each decade were:

1960's

  • A historic skate contest in Hermosa Beach, California, where a group of surfers (called the Z-boys) showed a completely new set of skills no one had seen at the time.
  • A new design of the skateboard, including a "kicktail".
  • New style of skating was created. Skating in empty backyard pools to simulate waves was performed, creating a precursor to styles such as "park", "bowl", and "vert".

1970's

  • Key new design feature of the skateboard, invention and use of urethane wheels.
  • New design features include wider boards, more concave shape, and a prominent nose and tail.
  • Structures were being built with skateboarding in mind, giving birth to the "skate park".
  • A new trick performed called the "ollie", which allows a rider to make their skateboard leace the ground, expanded the scope of skateboarding to allow for an entire new world of manoeuvers and styles.

1980's

  • The increasing roster of tricks and styles skateboarding was developing, also spurred on other sports to take it in a similar direction. Such as BMX, Inline Skating (aggressive sakting) and also a new style of skateboarding was emerging, called "street".
  • Thrasher magazine was created and became a central influencer in promoting skateboarding and shaping the culture, as well as increasing its popularity.
  • The first professional skateboarders were created as the market for skateboarding was growing. The first companies to sponsor riders were Powell Peralta, Santa Cruz and Vision.

1990's (and onwards)

  • The "Street" scene of skateboarding took off in the 90's to become its predominant style for the culture
  • Bigger and more succesful competitions were formed and grew around skateboarding such as Street League and X-Games which garnered a wider audience.

Skateboard Anatomy

The skateboard is made up of:


  • The Deck:

    • The largest component of the skateboard, coming in all different shapes and sizes, and made up of layered, compressed sheets of wood. This creates the platform for the rider to stand on.


  • Grip Tape:

    • Grip Tape is a sheet of sand-paper type material that sticks to the deck, giving the rider a rough surface for their feet to "grip" the board to assist with manoeuvers.


  • Bolts and Nuts (Hardware):

    • The Bolts and Nuts (also known as "Hardware") go through the deck and screw on to the trucks to attach it to the deck.


  • The Trucks:

    • The Trucks are the next biggest component after the deck. The Trucks are made up of smaller compenents, such as the baseplate, the kingpin, hanger, bushings and axels. The Trucks are attached to the board and created a frame for the wheels to roll on and for the board to flex.


  • The Wheels (and extended Hardware):

    • Skateboard wheels come in diffrent shapes, sizes and hardeness (different material), to suit the rest of the skateboard and the style of the rider. The wheels have bearings on both sides for it to roll on the axel, a spacer in between, with washers and nuts to fasten the wheels to the axels on the trucks.
